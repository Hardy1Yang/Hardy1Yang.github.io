Dissertation

Precision and Robustness in Adaptive Testing: An Item Response Theory Analysis within an Online Convex Optimization Framework

Advisor: Prof. Yung-Fong Hsu · Completed: July 2026

Computerized adaptive testing (CAT) assigns items adaptively to estimate ability with as few items as possible. Despite a rich asymptotic literature, a framework for understanding finite-sample behavior has been missing. This dissertation brings the Online Convex Optimization (OCO) framework to CAT, organized around two complementary themes.

Precision
Shows that the classical information-maximization method is a no-regret algorithm under the OCO framework, and constructs anytime-valid confidence intervals via martingale concentration inequalities and confidence sequences.
Robustness
Designs stability-oriented item-selection algorithms — adapting Upper Confidence Bound (UCB) methods from the multi-armed bandit literature — that account for early-stage estimation error, premature commitment bias, and item-parameter uncertainty during online calibration.

    Investigates interactions among individuals, families, and the market under health shocks and long-term care demands. I analyze government administrative data to study wage inequality in Taiwan with the Abowd–Kramarz–Margolis (AKM) framework, and intra-household decision-making and tax-filing strategies using comprehensive income-tax records, complemented by surveys and field experiments.

I review the IRT literature, conduct algorithmic research, and implement the algorithms; we are developing a Bayesian state-space framework to estimate user ability and account for non-linear improvement over time.
